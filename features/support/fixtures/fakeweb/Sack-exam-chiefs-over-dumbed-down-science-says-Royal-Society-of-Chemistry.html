'Sack exam chiefs over dumbed-down science' says Royal Society of Chemistry

By Laura Clark
Last updated at 7:57 AM on 06th November 2009

  • Comments (24)
  • Add to My Stories

Exam boards should be fined or sacked for dumbing down science GCSEs and A-levels as they 'race to the bottom' to win business from schools, according to the Royal Society of Chemistry.

Warning that the exam system was 'not working', it demanded tougher penalties for boards that undermine standards.

Chief executive Dr Richard Pike fears they are diluting the difficulty of papers as they compete to sell their services.

Exam boards should be fined or sacked for dumbing down science GCSEs and A-levels, according to the Royal Society of Chemistry (photo posed by model)

He said a study this year had highlighted examples of GCSE science questions requiring neither scientific knowledge nor a grasp of maths.

Dr Pike insisted such a 'blatant' breach of regulations deserved severe penalties, adding: 'Break the rules in Formula 1 and you get banned. Contravene competition law and you get fined.

'A £1million surcharge would focus the mind of any examining board chief executive and overnight would do more than years of "discussion between stakeholders".'


His call follows the news that the watchdog Ofqual told boards to make exams harder after science standards were compromised by GCSE reforms in 2006.

He warned that boards were increasingly focusing on ' simplicity and multiple- choice questions', adding: 'Evidence gathered recently by the science community has identified entire science papers with no underlying mathematics, and science questions with no science.

'This is a blatant breach of standards. As examining boards compete to make their wares more attractive to schools and pupils, it really is a race to the bottom, with each one pushing the boundaries set by the regulators and sometimes going right through them.'

Dr Pike said boards should face tougher supervision.

He added: 'The key to this is a truly independent regulator with teeth, coupled with transparency, honesty and a willingness to challenge an examination system that is not working.

'Only then will we be able to resolve the skills crisis and provide great career opportunities.'
